# Hunting Accident

A hunter is injured in a remote location — gunshot wound, fall from a tree stand, or medical emergency. They may be alone or with a partner who can't leave them. Cell service is typically absent. The search area is defined by "they were hunting somewhere in this national forest" — thousands of acres.

> **[[Hunting accident|Hunting Accident]] — What to do immediately**
> - Activate Beacon — single-button, silent activation. No noise to spook game or attract unwanted attention
> - If in a tree stand, hang Beacon from the stand above you — elevated position improves visibility
> - If your hunting partner leaves to get help, they can guide rescuers back by the strobe
> - The dark polycarbonate cover is non-reflective when off — compatible with hunting equipment
> - 26+ hour runtime spans the response window in remote hunting areas — some zones are 12-24 hours from help

## The Scenario

Hunting accidents are low-frequency but high-consequence. The victim is often camouflaged (blaze orange isn't universal), making visual search difficult. They may be unable to move. Their hunting partner may need to leave them to get help — and then try to guide rescuers back to an unmarked location in dense forest.

## Current Technologies & Practices

| Technology | Used By | Strengths | Gap |
|------------|---------|-----------|-----|
| Cell phone | Universal¹ | Two-way communication, GPS pin drop | No service in most hunting areas. Battery dies in cold |
| Whistle | BSA, hunter safety² | Lightweight, audible signal | Range ~0.1 mile in forest; requires conscious victim |
| Signal mirror | Universal | Daylight passive | Useless at night, under canopy, in overcast — most hunting incidents occur at dawn/dusk |
| Flashlight / headlamp | Universal³ | Illumination, can be waved for signal | Directional beam — must be aimed at rescuer; batteries die in cold |
| Hunting GPS / InReach | Backcountry hunters⁴ | Two-way satellite messaging, SOS | $300+ device + subscription. Minority carry. Battery-dependent |
| Tree stand harness | Treestand hunters⁵ | Prevents fall death from stand | Suspension trauma if hanging — rapid loss of consciousness. No signaling function |

## The Gap

A hunter down in dense forest at dusk is invisible. Camouflage — the thing that keeps them hidden from game — keeps them hidden from rescuers. A strobe defeats camouflage on command: invisible when off, unmistakable when activated. The silent activation means they can signal without spooking game or alerting a threat. The 26-hour runtime spans the entire response window — SAR in remote hunting areas can take 12-24+ hours.

## Beacon Design Response

Single-button press-and-hold activation — silent operation. White SOS strobe at maximum brightness visible from air through partial canopy. 26+ hour runtime spans remote-area SAR response window. When off: dark polycarbonate cover and recessed bars present a low-profile, non-reflective surface. IR mode for NVG-equipped SAR aircraft — invisible to game, visible to rescuers. Compact — threads onto pack strap, belt loop, or harness webbing.

## Beacon Perspective

Beacon doesn't call for help. Someone has to know you're missing. The strobe makes you findable — it doesn't start the search.

**Where Beacon falls short:** In triple-canopy forest with no gaps to the sky, the strobe is attenuated to ground-level only — visible from within the forest, not from aircraft. It doesn't transmit GPS coordinates. It won't help if no one knows where to look — a hunt plan left with someone is still the trigger that starts SAR. A satellite messenger can send an SOS with GPS from anywhere with sky view; Beacon can't. They're complementary, not competing.

## Coalition Footnotes

[¹] **Hunter safety organizations (IHEA, state DNR):** Hunter education curriculum includes emergency preparedness. Standard advice: leave hunt plan with someone, carry communication device. Gap: no dedicated emergency strobe in standard hunter equipment recommendations.

[²] **BSA / Scout troops:** Many hunters were scouts. 10 Essentials carry-over to hunting kit is common but incomplete — flashlight included, strobe not. Gap in signaling capability between "wave flashlight" and "satellite messenger." [[SAR Coalition partner benefits]]

[³] **Outfitters / guides:** Professional hunting guides carry satellite communication (InReach/SPOT) and first aid. Client equipment is variable. Guide's kit may not include a strobe distributable to an injured client separated from the group.

[⁴] **Garmin InReach / SPOT:** Satellite messengers with SOS and two-way texting. Growing adoption among backcountry hunters. Limitations: $300+ device + monthly subscription. Battery life measured in days, but device requires conscious operation — injured, hypothermic hunter may be unable to operate touchscreen.

[⁵] **Treestand Manufacturer Association (TMA):** Harness standard requires full-body fall arrest. Suspension trauma: hanging motionless in harness causes blood pooling, loss of consciousness in <30 minutes. A strobe on the harness — activated by fall or manually — turns an unconscious suspended hunter into an active signal for the hunting partner returning to the stand.
