# USCG — Boat Crew Operations

**Source:** Boat Crew Handbook — Boat Operations, BCH16114.1. USCG training materials. Chapter 6: Air Operations (Helicopter Rescue), Chapter 2: Boat Crew Duties (Lookout, NVG, Thermal Imaging).

## Night Vision and Thermal Imaging

From Chapter 2, Section D (Boat Crewmember Responsibilities):

> D.19. Night Vision Devices (AN/PVS-14 MNVD) — Standard-issue night vision monocular used by USCG boat crews for nighttime operations. Enables passive visual acquisition in low-light conditions.

> D.20. Thermal Imaging Devices — Forward-looking infrared systems that detect temperature differences. Available in white-hot and black-hot display modes. Used for detecting persons in water, vessels without running lights, and navigation hazards at night.

> Figure 2-3: White Hot Thermal Display
> Figure 2-4: Black Hot Thermal Display

The Boat Crew Handbook explicitly trains crews on both NVG and thermal imaging for nighttime SAR operations. An IR strobe at 850nm is directly visible through both systems — the NVG detects the near-IR emission, and the thermal imager detects the LED junction temperature contrast against the water background.

## Fog and Restricted Visibility

From Chapter 2, Section D:

> D.13. Fog Scanning — Specialized lookout technique for restricted visibility conditions. Fog distorts and scatters light, requiring modified scanning patterns.
>
> D.18. Night Fog — Combined darkness and fog. The most challenging visual search condition. Lookouts trained to use available light sources and NVG to penetrate fog layers.

These procedures acknowledge the fundamental limitation: in fog, passive visual search is severely degraded. An active strobe that penetrates fog (white LED) or operates in wavelengths less scattered by water droplets (IR) provides a detection modality that complements trained lookout technique.

## Helicopter Rescue Operations

From Chapter 6, Sections A through C (Air Operations):

The Boat Crew Handbook dedicates an entire chapter to helicopter operations, including:

- **Section A: Helicopters and Equipment** — MH-60T Jayhawk capabilities, hoist equipment, rescue basket, rescue swimmer deployment
- **Section B: Helicopter Rescue Operations** — Helicopter-boat positioning, course and speed, dead-in-the-water procedures, delivery of rescue device, direct delivery, hoist procedures
- **Section C: Helicopter Ditching** — Emergency procedures for helicopter water landing, crew egress, life raft deployment

> Helicopter-Boat Positioning: The coxswain must position the boat relative to the helicopter to provide a stable hoist platform, accounting for rotor wash, sea state, and the helicopter crew's visual reference.

The hoist operator's visual acquisition of the victim is the critical first step in any helicopter rescue. An IR strobe visible through NVG provides positive identification before the aircraft commits to a hover position.

## Lookout Positioning and Scanning

From Chapter 2, Section D.4:

> Lookouts should be strategically placed to account for different hull shapes (maximum beam, tumble-home, flare) or appendages (rub rails, spray rails). Duties other than lookout should be kept to a minimum.

> During nighttime operations, when a light must be used, use a red light. Avoid using flash cameras or mobile phones with flash capabilities.

The lookout is the final element in the SAR detection chain. All technology — Rescue 21 triangulation, SAROPS drift modeling, optimized search patterns — converges on a crew member scanning water with their eyes. An active strobe on the victim changes what the lookout is searching FOR, from a passive target to an active signal.

## Person in Water Recovery

From Chapter 4 (Person in the Water Recovery):

> A.26. Person Overboard is Uninjured and Conscious — Approach procedures, recovery methods, and crew coordination for PIW recovery.

> All options must be considered, keeping in mind that a person suffering from hypothermia and/or exhaustion may not be able to assist when being pulled from the water.

Hypothermia and exhaustion render the victim passive — they cannot wave, shout, or assist in their own recovery. The strobe must work without ongoing user action. Beacon's single-button activation (press and hold once) and 26-hour autonomous operation addresses this requirement.
