# BSA Lifesaving

**Source:** *Lifesaving*, Scouting America Merit Badge Series, 2026 printing, 96 pages. ISBN 978-0-8395-3297-2.

## Rescuer Safety First

> Rule No. 1: Don't become victim No. 2. Some people drown in futile attempts to save others, but those would-be rescuers are usually frantic friends or relatives whose swimming skills are little better than those of the person in distress. They lack basic lifesaving training and act inappropriately.

> Do not risk injury to yourself when a rescue appears futile. Go for help rather than going into the water.

**Beacon relevance:** Rescuer safety is the cardinal rule — and it reinforces why signaling matters before rescue. A subject who can signal for help doesn't need a bystander to attempt a dangerous water entry. Beacon enables the subject to summon professional rescuers rather than forcing untrained bystanders into the water.

## The Rescue Hierarchy: REACH–THROW–ROW–GO

> The order of preference for rescue techniques, from simple to more complex, is easy to remember: REACH–THROW–ROW–GO. The method you select will depend on the victim's condition and distance from shore, available aids, and the condition of the water.

**Beacon relevance:** The REACH-THROW-ROW-GO hierarchy is the water rescue ladder. Beacon doesn't perform any of these — it operates BEFORE them. Beacon signals "I am here" so that rescuers can REACH or THROW from shore rather than needing to ROW or GO. Every step up the ladder increases risk to the rescuer. Beacon aims to keep the rescue at the lowest rung.

## Paddlesport Drowning Statistics

> According to the American Canoe Association, 85 percent of canoeing victims and 48 percent of kayaking victims were not wearing life jackets. Unfortunately, they could not swim well enough at the time to save themselves.

**Beacon relevance:** This is direct data supporting Beacon's primary market. Paddlesports — canoeing and kayaking — are high-risk activities where victims are often not wearing PFDs and cannot self-rescue. Beacon is designed to attach to a PFD. A paddler wearing both a PFD and Beacon converts from "victim who can't swim to safety" to "victim who can signal for rescue while floating."

## Reaching Rescues

> Reaching rescues are safe, simple, and effective. If you are out of the water and spot someone in trouble, lie down at the edge, extend a hand, and pull the victim to safety. With reaching rescues, always lie down first or otherwise brace yourself.

> Provide verbal instruction and assurance to all conscious victims.

**Beacon relevance:** A reaching rescue requires the rescuer to SEE the victim — to spot them in the water. At night, in fog, in waves, a person's head is nearly invisible at any distance. A strobe makes the victim visible, enabling the reaching rescue that would otherwise be impossible because the rescuer can't find them.

## Throwing Rescues

> For victims beyond your reach, an aid may be thrown from shore. A floating aid with a line attached is best because it provides support and allows you to pull the victim in. Always keep track of a victim's location. If the victim submerges, you need to know where he or she went down. Work with a partner if possible. One person can act as a spotter while the other gathers equipment and performs the rescue.

> Active drowning victims cannot reach for aids.

**Beacon relevance:** "Always keep track of a victim's location" — this is the visual tracking problem Beacon solves. A spotter who loses visual contact with a victim can't direct a throw. A strobe maintains visual contact even in poor conditions. The fact that "active drowning victims cannot reach for aids" underscores that Beacon's strobe is the right tool: it doesn't require the victim to do anything except have it attached and activated. The strobe does the work.

## Pinpointing a Victim's Location — Visual Bearings

> The farther away you are, the harder it will be to keep track of where the person went down. As you approach, line up the victim with two marks on the shore, a shorter one in front of a taller one. Two fixed points are required to define the line. The only sure way to pinpoint a location on the water is for two or more people to align objects with the victim from different locations. The spot where the lines of sight cross is where the person went down.

**Beacon relevance:** This bearing technique depends entirely on continuous visual contact. A victim who submerges before bearings are taken is lost. A strobe provides continuous visual reference — the spotter can maintain the bearing as they approach, and can re-acquire it if lost. At night, the strobe is the bearing point itself; no shore objects needed.

## Rowing Rescues — Motorboat Safety

> With motorboats, cut the engine before the final approach to avoid injuries from the prop. If only single-person kayaks are available, consider launching two kayaks for the rescue rather than one. Keep the victim in sight at all times. Shout encouragement and instructions to the victim.

**Beacon relevance:** "Keep the victim in sight at all times" is the constant refrain across every rescue method. The strobe makes this possible. In rough water, at distance, in low light — the strobe is what the boat crew tracks.

## The Four A's of Rescue

> Recall the four A's appropriate for any rescue: Awareness, Assessment, Action, and Aftercare.

**Beacon relevance:** Beacon operates at the Awareness stage — it makes rescuers aware that a victim exists and where they are. Without Awareness, the other three A's never activate.

## Noncontact vs. Contact Rescues

> In noncontact rescues, the victim grasps the rescue aid you provide. Noncontact rescues are the first choice for active victims. In contact rescues, you grasp the victim and the aid. Contact rescues are normally required for unconscious victims.

**Beacon relevance:** A conscious victim with Beacon can be located via strobe — a noncontact rescue becomes possible because the rescuer can approach from a distance guided by light. For an unconscious victim, Beacon still marks position — enabling a contact rescue with a known target rather than an expanding search area.
