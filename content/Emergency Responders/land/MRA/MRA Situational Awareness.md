# MRA Situational Awareness in Mountain Rescue

**Source:** "Situational Awareness in Mountain Rescue" by Charley Shimanski, Mountain Rescue Association, © 2017.

## Definition

> "Situational Awareness" is "the degree of accuracy by which one's perception of his/her current environment mirrors reality." The essence of Situational Awareness is fairly simple… perception vs. reality.

> Situational Awareness is not an event, but rather a process that only ends when the SAR incident is concluded.

**Beacon relevance:** Situational awareness applies to the lost subject too. A person who knows they're visible (strobe active) behaves differently than one who feels invisible. SA is a two-sided problem — the searcher's awareness and the subject's awareness of being findable.

## The Three Stages

> 1. The perception of the relevant information
> 2. The comprehension and interpretation of that information
> 3. The projection of their states into the future

### Stage I — Perception

> In conventional search missions, rescuers are looking for CLUES more than they are looking for the missing subject. Why? Simply because there are far more clues than there are missing subjects, and by finding and following clues, one can find the missing subject much more quickly.

> Many accidents in search and rescue operations result from a series of different things happening. There are often a number of contributing factors that, if occurring individually, might not have resulted in an accident.

**Beacon relevance:** A strobe is the ultimate clue — it doesn't need to be found, it announces itself. Where searchers spend hours looking for footprints, broken branches, and other passive clues, an active strobe says "here I am" to every searcher in visual range simultaneously. This collapses Stage I (perception) to near-zero time.

### Stage II — Comprehension Requires Training

> The key to this stage of Situational Awareness is that it requires one to have and utilize key training and experience. For example, a rescuer in a high mountain rescue might have already perceived that the temperature is very hot. Still, without proper training in helicopter management, that rescuer might not be able to interpret that the high temperatures will have an effect on the rescue team's use of helicopter resources.

### Stage III — Projection

> If you want to know if it is going to rain, you don't look for rain, you look for CLOUDS. If you look for rain alone you'll only know that rain is coming at the very moment that it arrives. Looking for rain alone would mean that you are only OBSERVING and INTERPRETING, but not PROJECTING into the future.

> Visualize While En Route to a Call. In many emergency medical training programs, students are taught the value of visualizing the scenario prior to arriving at the rescue call.

## Seven Factors That Reduce Situational Awareness (US Navy)

> 1. Insufficient or poorly communicated information and communication
> 2. Fatigue and stress
> 3. Task underload
> 4. Task overload
> 5. Group mindset
> 6. "Press on Regardless" philosophy
> 7. Degrading operating conditions

### Task Underload

> Task underload was a factor in a fatal plane crash during a search operation in Colorado in 1988. While flying at their assigned altitude of 13,000 feet during a search, the plane's pilot told the spotter that he was bored, and that they were going to fly at 11,000 feet. He descended to this lower altitude. Shortly thereafter, the plane crashed in a downdraft.

**Beacon relevance:** Task underload during grid searching is exactly when a strobe flash catches attention. A flashing light breaks the monotony — it's an attention anchor that counteracts the boredom factor that degrades search effectiveness.

### Group Mindset and "Press on Regardless"

> An accident investigation board was assembled in 1967 after the Apollo I disaster in which Gus Grissom, Edward White, and Roger Chaffee were killed in a fire aboard the spacecraft. In assembling their list of contributing factors, the Board coined a new phrase: "Go Fever."

> "The decision to delay a shuttle launch had developed into an 'unwanted' decision by the members of the Shuttle team."

## Overconfidence Based on Experience

> Dr. Ken Kamler hypothesized from the 1996 Mt. Everest tragedy that some of the climbers were lulled into a false sense of security on the mountain, due in large part to the fact that they had successfully climbed Mt. Everest many times before. "Familiarization, and prolonged exposure without incident, leads to a loss of appreciation of risk."

> Most avalanche victims have had avalanche training. It is not the novice who is normally caught in an avalanche, but rather the experienced and educated winter backcountry traveler.

**Beacon relevance:** Experienced outdoor users are the most likely to need rescue — and the most likely to underestimate their risk. Beacon's target market isn't just beginners; it's the experienced paddler who "doesn't need safety gear" because they've never had an incident. The familiarity-risk paradox makes experienced users the highest-value segment.

## Common Errors by Stage

| Stage | Error |
|-------|-------|
| I — Perception | Too much/too little information, failure to review, too much stress |
| II — Comprehension | Too little experience, using previous experience incorrectly, misinterpreting data |
| III — Projection | Doing things the same way over and over, not questioning what you're doing |

> "You need to be always questioning what you're doing. This is especially true in SAR operations where your expectations are not being met."

## Ten Ways to Avoid Loss of SA (US Navy)

> 1. Actively question and evaluate your mission progress
> 2. Analyze your situation
> 3. Update and revise your image of the mission
> 4. Use assertive behaviors when necessary
> 5. Make suggestions
> 6. Provide relevant information without being asked
> 7. Ask questions as necessary
> 8. Confront ambiguities
> 9. State opinion on decisions/procedures
> 10. Refuse unreasonable requests

## Break the Chain

> Most accidents occur when a number of elements go wrong and compound upon each other.

> In a search for a missing party, rescuers suddenly asked themselves if they would change their search methodology if they presumed that the missing subject was suicidal. They made the appropriate changes, and indeed found the subject within a matter of a few hours.

**Beacon relevance:** The "break the chain" concept maps to the lost person's decision chain: delay calling for help → move from last known position → night falls → searchers expand grid → hours compound. A strobe breaks the chain at any point — the moment it's activated, the search area stops expanding.

## Useful Tips

> 1. If something doesn't look or feel right, then it probably isn't right
> 2. Watch out when you are busy or bored
> 3. Old habits are hard to break
> 4. Expectations can reduce awareness
> 5. Things that take longer are less likely to get done right
> 6. It's hard to detect something that isn't there
> 7. Distraction comes in many forms
> 8. Don't rely on reliable systems
> 9. Don't get too excited if an emergency occurs. That's when you'll make poor decisions
> 10. Prevention is the best way to avoid emergencies, and Situational Awareness is one of the best forms of prevention there is
