# Alaska Cold Injuries Guidelines (2014)

**Source:** "State of Alaska Cold Injuries Guidelines" — developed by an invited expert panel at the Southeast Region EMS Council Conference on Environmental Injuries, Sitka, Alaska. Lead authors: Ken Zafren, MD (State of Alaska Emergency Programs Medical Director) and Gordon Giesbrecht, PhD (Professor of Thermophysiology, University of Manitoba). Panel included USCG Flight Surgeon CDR Leslie Wood, MD.

## Levels of Hypothermia

> Accidental hypothermia is defined as the unintentional drop of core temperature to 35°C (95°F) or below.

| Level | Core Temp | Clinical Signs |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| Mild | 35-32°C (95-90°F) | Alert, shivering, normal/increased vitals |
| Moderate | 32-28°C (90-82°F) | Decreased LOC, slow heart/respiratory rate, shivering may stop below ~30°C |
| Severe | <28°C (<82°F) | Markedly decreased LOC, decreased/absent response to stimuli |

**Beacon relevance:** The transition from mild to moderate hypothermia — where shivering stops and mental status declines — is the point at which self-rescue becomes impossible. A person who can still press a button at 34°C may be unable to do so at 31°C. Beacon's single-press activation is designed for this window: it must be activatable with the last functional motor control a hypothermic person has.

## Handling Hypothermic Patients

> A patient with moderate to severe hypothermia should be handled very gently and kept horizontal if at all possible. When cold, the heart is very prone to ventricular fibrillation (VF) with any disturbance. Even cautious movement of a patient may induce VF.

> A severely hypothermic patient may be in a state of "suspended animation" or in a "metabolic icebox." Severely hypothermic patients have been known to survive neurologically intact for long periods of time even if they are in asystole.

**Beacon relevance:** "Handle gently" — this is the medical basis for ergonomic design. A hypothermic person with cold, stiff fingers can't manipulate small switches, complex interfaces, or multi-step sequences. The activation must be gross motor: one button, one press, unambiguous feedback.

## Cold Water Drowning

### Survival Time

> The 1-10-1 Principle of Cold Water Immersion:
> - **1 minute** — Cold shock: gasping, hyperventilation, panic. Get breathing under control.
> - **10 minutes** — Cold incapacitation: loss of effective use of fingers, arms, and legs. Perform critical actions before this window closes.
> - **1 hour** — Hypothermia: unconsciousness from deep body cooling. Even then, survival is possible for much longer in cold water.

**Beacon relevance:** The 10-minute window for functional movement is Beacon's design constraint. A paddler who capsizes in cold water has approximately 10 minutes of useful hand function. Beacon must be activatable in under 3 seconds by a person with numbing hands — before cold incapacitation closes the window. The device's large button, one-press operation, and bar-based attachment (no threading required) are direct responses to this constraint.

### Amazing Resuscitations

> - Lowest recorded core temperature with survival: 13.7°C (56.7°F). Patient made an excellent neurologic recovery.
> - Longest cardiac arrest with survival: 8 hours 40 minutes. CPR for 4 hours 49 minutes.
> - Longest submersion with complete neurologic recovery: 66 minutes in a 2.5 year-old child. Core temperature 19°C (66°F).

> Assume that a hypothermic patient can be resuscitated even if there is an absence of vital signs or any sign of life and the pupils are fixed and dilated.

**Beacon relevance:** The "no one is dead until warm and dead" doctrine means SAR teams will continue searching long after conventional timelines suggest futility. A strobe that continues to flash for 26+ hours (Beacon's battery life) aligns with the extended resuscitation window — the signal persists as long as there's any chance of survival.

## Avalanche Burial

> Asphyxia is the major cause of death in avalanche burial. If the airway is patent, survival is possible for extended periods.

> Only 50% of those buried survive after 30 minutes. Immediate companion rescue is critical.

**Beacon relevance:** An avalanche beacon (457 kHz transceiver) is the primary tool. Beacon complements it — if the victim is on the surface or partially buried, the strobe provides visual location within seconds. The IR component is detectable by helicopter FLIR even through light snow cover.

## Frostbite

> If frozen and rescue is near, keep frozen unless warm water thaw is available and no danger of refreezing. Refreezing after thawing causes catastrophic tissue damage.

> Rewarm in warm water bath: 37-39°C (99-102°F) for 20-45 minutes or until flushing occurs.

> Delay debridement or amputations for at least 21 days unless absolutely necessary. There is less tissue retraction after this time. Premature amputation may cause the loss of up to 3-5 cm.

**Beacon relevance:** Frostbite affects hands first — the very hands that need to activate a signaling device. The "keep frozen until definitive care" rule means a frostbitten person may be unable to self-rescue and must rely on signaling. Beacon's large button is designed for operation with numb, stiff, or frost-nipped fingers.

## Core Temperature Measurement in the Field

> A rapid assessment to suggest a patient's core temperature may be performed by placing a warm ungloved hand against the skin of a patient's back, or chest. If the skin feels warm, hypothermia is unlikely.

> Core temperature in an unconscious (intubated) hypothermic patient is best measured with an esophageal probe in the lower third of the esophagus.

> Rectal and bladder temperatures are very slow to respond to changes in core temperature. During rewarming, core temperature may be rising while rectal or bladder temperature is still falling.

## Helicopter Transport of Cold Patients

> When moving hypothermic patients by helicopter, protect the patient from wind chill caused by rotor wash. Rotor wash can be eliminated if the helicopter shuts down while loading and unloading, but this is usually not practical in the field and is very time consuming.

**Beacon relevance:** Rotor wash during helicopter extraction adds wind chill to an already hypothermic patient. An attached Beacon strobe continues flashing through the rotor wash — unaffected by wind, spray, or cold — while rescuers focus on packaging the patient. It's one less thing for the rescue team to manage.

## Alaska Drowning Data (Addendum)

> During 2007-2012, Alaska recorded 300 drowning deaths. Average 50 per year. 84% male. 22% occupational.

> The drowning death rate among children aged 0-9 years was 2.6 per 100,000 persons, a decrease of 45% from 4.7 per 100,000 in 2000-2006.

> 49% of occupational boating victims were not wearing a PFD at the time of incident. 56% of non-occupational boating victims known not to have worn a PFD.

> 43% of adult non-occupational drowning deaths suspected or confirmed to have involved alcohol.

**Beacon relevance:** The PFD non-wear rate (~50%) and alcohol involvement (~43%) are the two biggest modifiable risk factors. Beacon addresses what happens after the immersion — it doesn't prevent drowning but it dramatically improves the chance of being found by making the victim visible. This is the safety net for when the other prevention layers (PFD, sobriety) fail.
