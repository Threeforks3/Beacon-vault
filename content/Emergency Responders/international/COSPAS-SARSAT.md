# COSPAS-SARSAT

The International Satellite System for Search and Rescue. A satellite network that detects and locates 406 MHz emergency beacons (EPIRBs, ELTs, PLBs). COSPAS-SARSAT is the global standard for satellite-based distress alerting.

## How It Works

A 406 MHz beacon transmits a distress signal with GPS coordinates. MEOSAR satellites detect the alert within minutes. The position is relayed to a rescue coordination center, which dispatches SAR assets to the last known position.

**What it gives you:** a point-in-time GPS fix — the location where the beacon was when it transmitted. Accurate to within 1-3 nautical miles.

**What it doesn't give you:** a track. The fix is a snapshot, not a moving update. In water, the victim drifts away from that fix within minutes. On land, a lost person keeps walking. SAR arrives at the last known position and must then search visually.

## Beacon's Relationship

A Coast Guard responder carries four tools on duty: signal mirror, strobe light, whistle, EPIRB. They're complementary — each closes a different part of the search. The EPIRB brings rescuers near via satellite. The strobe marks position. The mirror catches sun. The whistle carries sound at close range.

Beacon is a mirror and SOS strobe in one — the civilian-side version of that kit. What you'd carry when you're off duty, on the water, and you're the one who needs to be found.

The 406 MHz alert gets SAR nearby. Beacon closes the last mile from the other side of the rescue.

**No 406 MHz integration planned.** Beacon operates in the visual/IR domain. Integrating 406 MHz would add cost, regulatory complexity, and false-alert risk. The satellite system and the visual strobe serve different phases of the same rescue.
