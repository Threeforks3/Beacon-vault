# Helicopter Hoist

A Coast Guard or military helicopter hovers above a victim — in water, on a vessel, or on terrain — and lowers a rescue swimmer or hoist cable. The victim must be visible to the hoist operator, and no equipment can interfere with the cable or create a snag hazard.

> **[[Helicopter hoist|Helicopter Hoist]] — What to do immediately**
> - Keep Beacon on — the hoist operator is looking straight down at you through NVG
> - IR mode preserves your night vision and the crew's — invisible to naked eye, unmistakable through NVG
> - Stay still — the strobe gives the operator a positive visual lock. Let the aircraft come to you
> - Nothing on your equipment should extend above your shoulders — snag hazard. Beacon's domed profile is snag-free
> - The bar-based attachment stays on through 60+ knot rotor wash. It will not separate

## The Scenario

The most dangerous phase of rescue: transitions. Moving from water to vessel, from vessel to aircraft, from ground to cable. Anything that tangles, snags, or distracts during a hoist can turn a rescue into a casualty.

Looking straight down from a hovering helicopter, a person in dark water or on dark terrain is nearly invisible. Rotor wash creates spray, dust, or snow — further reducing visibility. The hoist operator needs a POSITIVE visual lock on the victim throughout the entire hoist sequence. Crew of 4: pilot, copilot, flight mechanic (hoist operator), rescue swimmer. Hoist operator uses NVG for night operations.

## Current Technologies & Practices

| Technology | Used By | Strengths | Gap |
|------------|---------|-----------|-----|
| NVG visual acquisition | USCG, military¹ | Passive night vision, standard equipment | Passive — searching for a dark target. IR strobe makes target actively visible |
| FLIR targeting | USCG, military² | Thermal imaging from aircraft | Water surface masks thermal contrast; spray degrades image |
| Helicopter searchlight | USCG³ | Illuminates scene for visual acquisition | Floods scene with light — degrades crew NVG, disorients victim, visible for miles |
| Rescue swimmer | USCG⁴ | Deploys to victim, provides direct assistance | Swimmer must FIND victim first. Visual acquisition still required |
| Radio comms | USCG⁵ | Coordination between aircraft and surface units | Victim rarely has radio; surface unit may not be on scene |

## The Gap

Night hoist operations depend on NVG visual search — looking for a passive target in dark water. The helicopter must reach hover position, establish visual contact, and maintain it throughout the hoist. An IR strobe visible through NVG from 500ft+ allows the hoist operator to acquire the victim BEFORE the aircraft reaches hover — reducing hover time, fuel burn, and risk.

## Beacon Design Response

Overhead visibility: domed lens radiates upward — from directly above, the full dome is illuminated and SOS pattern visible even while victim spins on cable. Non-blinding: dome diffuses beam; at 30ft the strobe is bright but not disorienting. IR mode is invisible to naked eye — preserves crew and victim night adaptation. Snag-free: closed-bar architecture on cover back face is recessed — when chest-mounted on PFD, Beacon presents smooth dome forward. Rotor wash survival: tethered to PFD or flight suit. Dome shape sheds wind rather than catching it.

## Coalition Footnotes

[¹] **USCG Air Station Clearwater:** MH-60T Jayhawk crews fly with AN/AVS-9 NVG as standard night equipment. Hoist operator uses NVG throughout night hoist sequence. An IR strobe visible through NVG provides positive ID before hover — reducing time on station and fuel consumption. [[USCG approval pathway]] [[USCG SAR doctrine]]

[²] **USCG / Military:** FLIR pods on MH-60T and HC-130 provide wide-area thermal search. A human body in water is within 1-2°C of surrounding water — poor discrimination. An active IR strobe at 850nm is a bright point source — thermal contrast orders of magnitude higher than passive body heat.

[³] **USCG Night Operations:** Searchlight use is standard for visual confirmation but degrades crew NVG adaptation for minutes after deactivation. IR strobe eliminates the need for searchlight during acquisition phase — crew maintains NVG adaptation throughout.

[⁴] **USCG Rescue Swimmer:** Deployed from helicopter to assist victim. Swimmer must locate victim visually on descent. IR strobe visible through swimmer's NVG (if equipped) or visible strobe from surface provides positive vector.

[⁵] **USCG SAR Comms:** Air-to-surface coordination via VHF-FM and UHF. Victim rarely has operational radio at point of hoist. Visual signals (strobe, flare, dye) are primary location markers. [[USCG SAR doctrine]]
