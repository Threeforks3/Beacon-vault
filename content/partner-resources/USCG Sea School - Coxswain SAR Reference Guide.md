# USCG Sea School — Coxswain SAR Reference Guide

**Source:** A Guide for Boat Coxswains — Search and Rescue. 1st Edition, October 2006 (Updated January 2007). USCG Sea School training materials. Provided by USCG Auxiliary (D5-North, D7).

## Search Patterns

Each search pattern has a specific use. The SMC considers weather, search area size, search object size, number of SRUs available, search area location, and time limitations in deciding which pattern to use.

Patterns are designated by letters:
- First letter: pattern group (T=Trackline, P=Parallel, C=Creeping Line, V=Sector, S=Square)
- Second letter: number of units (S=Single, M=Multi)
- Third letter: specialized (R=Return, N=Non-return)

### Datum Marker

> When necessary, the SMC will instruct one of the SRU's to deploy a DMB to measure Total Water Current (TWC). If tasked to deploy a DMB, make sure you record and report the time and position where it was inserted. **You will typically use a fender or life ring with a strobe light as your DMB.**

The strobe light serves as the visual reference point for search pattern execution. This is a core operational use of strobes in USCG SAR — not just victim signaling, but search coordination.

### Expanding Square Search (SS)

> Used when the search area is small, the location of the search object is known within relatively close limits, but some doubt exists about the distress position, and a concentrated search is desired.
>
> The SRU goes to the best known location of the search object. This becomes the center (datum) of the search area. **Datum should be marked with a buoy, life ring, strobe light, etc.** The first leg will begin from datum in the direction of drift for one track space (S). All course changes are 90° to the right. Search leg length is increased by one track space on every other leg.

### Sector Search (VS)

> Used when the search area is small, the location of the search object is well known, and a concentrated search is desired.
>
> The SRU goes to the best known location of the search object. This becomes the center (datum) of the search area. **Datum should be marked with a buoy, life ring, strobe light, etc.** All course changes are 120° to the right and the length of all legs are equal to one S.

## Lookout Procedures

> Effective scanning by lookouts is accomplished with a series of short, regularly spaced eye movements that bring successive areas of the water into the central visual field. Each movement should not exceed 10 degrees. Each area should be observed for at least two seconds, plus time to refocus if necessary.

> Lookouts should be posted away from the engines and radios.

> Aural Searches — Surface SRU's should make every effort to reduce background noises, and occasionally stop the engines to permit faint calls for help to be heard.

## Visual Distress Signals

> A distressed vessel has a limited number of VDS's, if any at all, and experienced mariners usually do not activate these signals until they actually see or hear an SRU. You should consider the use of a searchlight to illuminate the distress scene and may draw the survivors' attention.

This operational reality has direct implications for Beacon: victims conserve signals until they perceive rescuers are close. A 26-hour strobe eliminates the conservation calculus — activate immediately, stay visible continuously.

## Rescue Planning

Situations where an SRU might not be able to perform rescue include:
- Too many survivors for one SRU to carry
- Weather conditions exceed SRU capabilities
- Locating the survivors by a fixed wing aircraft
- Distance from shore exceeds SRU capabilities

> Rescue planning includes: evaluating survivability of the incident, evaluating the environment, selecting the rescue method, selecting the rescue facilities, and developing the optimum and attainable rescue plan.

## Debriefing Survivors

> If the survivors were in the water for any length of time, have them describe in detail their emergency and survival procedures. This information might be helpful in later SAR cases.
