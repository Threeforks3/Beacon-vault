# Design Philosophy

Beacon was built from first principles — not by looking at what competitors do, but by asking what the problem actually demands.

## The Problem, Stated Clearly

A person in distress needs to be seen. They may be in the water, in the dark, in rough conditions. They may be injured, hypothermic, or losing consciousness. The window for rescue is measured in hours, sometimes minutes. Every design decision either widens that window or narrows it.

## First Principles

**1. The signal must outlast the search.** A 26-hour SOS strobe means the search can start at dusk and continue through the next night. Runtime wasn't a target — it was derived from SAR response timelines for remote areas.

**2. Activation must be possible under the worst conditions.** Cold hands. Wet hands. Gloved hands. One hand. No screen to read, no sequence to remember, no mode to cycle through. Press and hold. If you can press one button, you can activate Beacon.

**3. The device must survive what the person survives.** IP68 immersion. Impact from drops. Salt water corrosion. Through-hole components wave-soldered — no surface-mount parts to shear under G-loading. If a rescuer can reach you, Beacon is still working.

**4. Power must be universally available.** Two AAA alkaline batteries. Not a proprietary pack, not a rechargeable that needs a charger, not a lithium cell only sold online. You can buy AAAs at a marina, a gas station, a supermarket, anywhere in the world. On standby, they last years.

**5. The signal must be unambiguous.** SOS is the internationally recognized distress pattern. Not a generic strobe that could be a navigation light, a camera flash, or a shore marker. When someone sees SOS, they know what it means.

## Design for the Hardest

The method: design for the worst case. Cold water at night. Rough seas. Remote areas with delayed SAR response. If Beacon works in those conditions, it works in all of them.

This means the hard scenarios drive the requirements. Man overboard at night drives the strobe brightness. Paddlecraft separation drives the person-mounted design. Capsize in open water drives the waterproofing and floatation. Avalanche burial drives the impact resistance. The easy scenarios get solved for free.

## What Beacon Is Not

Beacon is not a satellite messenger. It doesn't call for help — it makes you findable once help is looking. It's not a PLB. It has no GPS, no radio transmitter, no subscription. It's a visual signal — the most direct, most universal, least infrastructure-dependent way to say "I am here."

A PLB starts the search. Beacon finishes it. They're complementary, and the distinction is deliberate.
