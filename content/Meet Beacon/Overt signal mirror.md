# Overt Signal Mirror

Beacon's mirror is the first signal mirror designed for overt emergency signaling — visible to anyone in the search grid, not targeted at a specific receiver. It inverts 80 years of signal mirror design.

## Two Mirrors, Two Missions

| Property | Covert Mirror (Standard) | Overt Mirror (Beacon) |
|---------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Geometry | Flat | Bent centerline — two angled planes |
| Sighting | Sighting hole for precision aiming | None — geometry spreads flash automatically |
| Hands | Two | One |
| Operator | Trained — hours of practice | Untrained — works immediately |
| Target | One specific receiver (Morse code) | Anyone in the search grid |
| Beam | Narrow (~0.53°), collimated | Wide, spread across SAR altitudes |
| Range | 50–100 miles (with skilled operator) | SAR-optimized (~3 miles from altitude) |
| Origin | Hunter 1946, NBS, WWII heliographs | Beacon 2026 — civilian distress |

## Why the Standard Mirror Is Mismatched

The standard signal mirror in every USCG kit, SOLAS life raft, and military survival vest descends directly from the WWII heliograph — a precision optical instrument for sending coded messages between ships and ground stations. Richard S. Hunter's 1946 study at the National Bureau of Standards optimized flat mirrors for maximum collimation and minimum dispersion. Even 0.020 inches of sagittal curvature was rejected as "unacceptable."

The USCG Boat Crew Handbook instructs crews to: "Reflect sunlight from the mirror onto a nearby surface... Slowly bring the mirror up to eye-level and look through the sighting hole... Hold the mirror near your eye and slowly turn and manipulate it." It then states: "Practice is the key to effective use of a signal mirror."

This is a precision instrument. It works brilliantly — for a trained operator, with two hands free, in calm conditions, signaling to a known target.

A capsized kayaker in cold water has none of those things.

## Beacon's Geometry

Beacon's mirror bends slightly along its centerline, creating two angled planar surfaces. Instead of collimating light into a narrow beam aimed at one receiver, the bend spreads the flash across a wider arc. An aircraft orbiting at 500–5,000 feet AGL — the standard Coast Guard search altitude envelope — passes through the flash cone without the victim ever aiming.

**Single-handed operation**: Hold it up. The geometry does the aiming. No sighting hole. No two-hand manipulation. No practice required.

**SAR-optimized range**: The bend reduces peak brightness compared to a flat mirror, but at SAR search altitudes the trade is decisive. A 50-mile signal to a specific receiver is useless if you can't find the receiver. A 3-mile flash visible from any direction catches every set of eyes in the search grid.

**Dual-purpose surface**: The mirror serves double duty. During the day, it reflects sunlight passively — no batteries, no electronics. At night, the LED strip runs along the bend axis, the mirror surface between the LEDs reflecting ambient light to supplement the active SOS.

## Limitations

- **Sun-dependent**: Requires direct sunlight. Useless at night (night is the LED's job), under heavy canopy, or with sun behind clouds at the wrong angle.
- **Not a precision instrument**: Cannot send Morse code. Cannot target a specific aircraft. The tradeoff is deliberate — overt vs covert.
- **Shorter range than flat**: The wider beam means lower peak intensity. At extreme range (>10 miles), a flat mirror in skilled hands outperforms Beacon's overt mirror.
- **Angle awareness**: User must face roughly toward the sun for the flash to project forward. The bend helps with angle tolerance but doesn't eliminate the need for approximate sun alignment.
