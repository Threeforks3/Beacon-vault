# Buoyancy

Beacon floats independently. If detached from PFD webbing or separated from the victim, the device floats lens-up — the heaviest component (battery) sits at the bottom, the LED dome points skyward. It continues signaling while adrift.

## Design

**Displacement vs mass:** The sealed enclosure displaces more water weight than the complete device mass (cover + body + PCB + battery + LED assembly). The center of mass is below the geometric center — the battery at the base, the LEDs at the dome — creating a self-righting moment in water.

**Orientation:** Lens-up flotation means the signal radiates upward — the direction search aircraft approach from. If Beacon is floating in the water, the strobe is visible from above, not pointed at the ocean floor.

## Operational Significance

**If the device separates from the victim:** In a capsize, swift water event, or avalanche tumble, equipment can be torn from webbing. Beacon's bar-based attachment is designed to survive these forces, but no attachment is absolute. If separation occurs, a floating, self-righting, actively-signaling device continues to mark the victim's last known position.

**As a thrown marker:** A rescuer can activate Beacon and throw it into the water to mark a position — a victim's last seen location, a submerged hazard, a search reference point. The device floats and signals, providing a persistent visual reference that doesn't require a rescuer to remain on station.

**In a life raft:** If Beacon is the only signaling device in a raft, it can be deployed outside the raft (tethered) to provide an elevated signal visible above wave height.

## Limitations

Beacon is NOT a PFD. It does not provide buoyancy to a person. It floats itself — not a victim. The buoyancy design is about signal persistence, not personal flotation.

## Open Questions

- Is the self-righting moment sufficient in rough seas (3+ foot waves)?
- Should a tether point be added for life raft deployment?
- Does cold water (denser) improve or degrade flotation margin?
