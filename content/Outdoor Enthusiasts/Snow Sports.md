# Snow Sports — Ski, Snowboard, Snowshoe, Snowmobile

## Float Plan

Backcountry snow travelers must file a trip plan with multiple contacts. The plan includes: zone, entry/exit routes, expected return window, group composition, vehicle description. Winter conditions compress the survival timeline — a stranded snow traveler has hours, not days.

- **MRA:** Avalanche terrain requires beacon, probe, shovel — minimum. Trip plan with avalanche forecast is mandatory.
- **Backcountry skiers:** Most carry avalanche safety gear. Few carry general-purpose signaling beyond cell phone.
- **Resort skiers/snowboarders:** Inside boundaries, ski patrol provides coverage. Out-of-bounds or tree-well incidents are invisible.

## Regs

- **Avalanche terrain:** Beacon (457 kHz), probe, shovel required by land managers on backcountry access gates
- **Resort:** No signaling requirements beyond helmet recommendations in some areas
- **Snowmobile:** Varies by state — some require headlight and taillight. No signaling requirements.

## Signals

| Tool | Range | Duration | Limitation |
|------|-------|----------|------------|
| Avalanche Beacon | 40-80m | ~24h | Short range, one purpose |
| Whistle | ~0.5nm | Unlimited | Audible — snow absorbs sound |
| Cell Phone | Cell-dependent | Battery-hours | Cold drains battery rapidly |
| Satellite Messenger | Global | Days | SOS button, subscription |
| PLB | Global | 24-48h | One-way alert |
| Strobe (Beacon) | 1-5nm | Duty-cycle | Visual — critical in whiteout |

**MRA data:** Cold weather reduces battery capacity by 30-50%. Any electronic signaling device must account for this. Beacon's battery chemistry selection is driven by cold performance — not all lithium chemistries perform equally below freezing.

## Environment

- **Cold:** Hypothermia onset is rapid in wet snow or wind. A stranded skier without shelter may have 2-4 hours before incapacitation.
- **Whiteout:** Disorientation is immediate. Without visual reference, a victim yards from safety may be invisible to rescuers.
- **Tree wells:** Deep snow pockets around trees — victim falls in, inverted, invisible, silent. Snow absorbs sound, so whistle may not be heard.
- **Avalanche burial:** Victim is pinned, immobile, unable to signal. Avalanche beacon and RECCO reflector are the only standard tools. An active visual/IR strobe on helmet or pack could mark location for above-snow acquisition.

## Community Gear

- **Backcountry skier/splitboarder:** Avalanche beacon, probe, shovel, airbag pack. Satellite messenger common. Whistle common on pack. Active strobe rare.
- **Resort skier/snowboarder:** Helmet (growing), goggles, phone in chest pocket. No signaling gear.
- **Snowmobiler:** Helmet, GPS, radio on group rides. Avalanche beacon in backcountry zones. Strobe uncommon.
- **Snowshoer/hiker:** Minimal gear — may carry whistle. Unlikely to have beacon, satellite device, or active strobe.

**Gap:** The backcountry community has excellent avalanche-specific gear but no general-purpose distress signal that works outside avalanche terrain. A skier who falls into a tree well, breaks a leg on a remote traverse, or gets separated from their group in whiteout has no way to mark their position continuously.
