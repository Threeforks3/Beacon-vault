# Outdoor Enthusiasts

SAR professionals train for rescue. The rest of us train to avoid needing one. But when things go wrong — a capsized kayak, a lost trail, a hunting accident miles from help — the gap between "I need help" and "they can see me" is where outcomes are decided.

This section is for paddlers, hikers, hunters, climbers, mountain bikers, and everyone else who goes outside. The perspective here is the person who needs to be found — not the responder, not the equipment evaluator. What you need to know. What you should carry. What actually works.

## What to Carry

The minimum signaling kit for any outdoor activity:

- **A light.** Not your phone. A dedicated strobe visible for miles. Your phone battery dies in hours; a strobe runs for 26+.
- **A mirror.** Passive, infinite endurance. Works when batteries don't.
- **A whistle.** Close range, no power needed. Packs smaller than a key.

These three cover day, night, close range, and long range. Everything else — satellite messengers, PLBs, VHF radios — adds capability on top of this foundation.

## How to Be Found

The most important thing: **activate your signal early.** The instinct is to wait until you hear rescuers. Don't. A strobe visible for 2+ miles gives SAR a target from the moment they enter the search area. Every minute you wait is a minute the search grid expands without a signal to converge on.

Second most important: **stay visible.** Keep the strobe at the highest point on your person or vessel. If you're in the water, keep it above the surface. If you're on land, elevate it — a branch, a pack, anything that gets it above ground clutter.

Third: **conserve yourself.** Don't swim if you can float. Don't walk if you can stay put. The strobe does the work of being seen — you do the work of staying alive until help arrives.
