# Hunting — Rifle, Bow, Waterfowl

## Float Plan

Hunters often hunt alone or in small parties spread across terrain. Trip plan should include hunting zone, vehicle location, expected return, and emergency contact. The most common hunting SAR scenario: a hunter who did not return at dark.

- **BSA:** Hunter education courses teach trip planning as core safety. Signaling is taught as emergency skill — whistle, mirror, three shots.
- **MRA:** Hunters are a significant SAR demographic in fall months. Most incidents involve hunters who deviated from their planned zone, became disoriented after dark, or were injured and could not self-extricate.

## Regs

- **Hunter education:** Required in all states. Signaling (three shots, whistle, mirror) is taught in every course.
- **Blaze orange:** Required in most states during firearm seasons. Provides passive visual identification but does not function as distress signal.
- **Waterfowl:** Camouflage requirements conflict with visibility — intentionally hard to see. Blaze orange required during transit but not in blind.

## Signals

| Tool | Range | Duration | Limitation |
|------|-------|----------|------------|
| Firearm (3 shots) | ~1nm | Limited ammo | Depends on ammunition, may not be heard |
| Whistle | ~0.5nm | Unlimited | Audible — terrain blocks |
| Cell Phone | Cell-dependent | Battery | Backcountry coverage gaps |
| Satellite Messenger | Global | Days | SOS, subscription |
| Signal Mirror | 10-50nm | Unlimited | Sun-dependent |
| Strobe (Beacon) | 1-5nm | Duty-cycle | Visual — complements three-shot signal |

**BSA doctrine:** "Three of anything — visual or audible — means distress." A whistle and signal mirror are mandatory SAR gear in BSA Search and Rescue pamphlet. An active strobe extends this principle to night and low-visibility conditions where mirror and visual hand signals fail.

## Environment

- **Remote:** Hunters operate far from roads in terrain without cell coverage. Access is often by ATV or foot — injury means hours to extraction.
- **Cold:** Late-season hunts in snow and freezing temperatures. Hypothermia and frostbite risks. Cold drains electronics.
- **Dark:** Hunters are stationary at dawn/dusk — peak activity times. Shot in last light means extraction in darkness.
- **Tree stand:** Falls from elevated stands are the most common hunting injury. Victim on ground, often alone, immobile.

## Community Gear

- **Rifle hunter:** Firearm, orange vest/hat, knife, GPS/handheld, game bags. Whistle common. Signal mirror uncommon. Active strobe rare.
- **Bow hunter:** Camouflage, bow/arrows, rangefinder, tree stand or ground blind. Whistle on pack strap. No active electronic signaling.
- **Waterfowl hunter:** Shotgun, decoys, waders, dog. VHF radio sometimes carried for group coordination. No distress-specific signaling beyond voice.
- **Public land hunter:** GPS, phone, knife, first aid. Often hunts alone. Leaves vehicle location with spouse but no formal float plan.

**Gap:** The hunting community carries firearms which double as audible signals (three shots). But an injured hunter who cannot fire their weapon, or a hunter who has discharged all ammunition, has no fallback. A power-independent active strobe closes this gap — it works whether the hunter can move, shoot, or speak.
