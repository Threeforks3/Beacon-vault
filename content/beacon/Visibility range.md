# Visibility Range

Beacon's white strobe is visible from 2-3+ nautical miles at night from search altitude. The SOS pattern is distinctive — immediately recognizable as a distress signal, not a navigation light or shore marker.

## Distance Factors

Visibility depends on: LED brightness, atmospheric conditions, observer altitude, and background light competition.

**At 500ft search altitude** (helicopter pattern): 2-3 nautical miles in clear conditions, 1-2 nautical miles in light precipitation, 0.5-1 nautical mile in heavy rain or fog.

**At 1,500ft search altitude** (fixed-wing pattern): 3-5 nautical miles in clear conditions. The higher altitude provides longer optical path through cleaner air.

**From surface vessels:** 1-3 nautical miles depending on swell height and observer elevation. Larger vessels with higher bridges see further.

**In forest canopy:** At night, visible through 10-20% canopy gaps at 1-2 miles from aircraft. Ground teams at closer range can see the strobe through dense forest by the light cast on surrounding foliage.

## Daytime

Daytime visibility is inherently limited — a white strobe doesn't compete with sunlight. Beacon is primarily a night and low-light signaling device. For daytime search, passive markers (orange PFD, signal mirror, dye marker) complement the active strobe.

## IR Range

Through night vision goggles, the IR strobe at 850nm is visible at similar or greater ranges than the white strobe — NVG systems amplify IR, and the background is uniformly dark. At 3+ nautical miles from altitude, an IR strobe through NVG is a brilliant flashing point source.

## SOS Distinctiveness

The SOS pattern (... --- ...) is internationally recognized as a distress signal. It is encoded in the ITU Radio Regulations, the International Code of Signals, and the International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea (COLREGs). No navigation light, recreational strobe, or shore marker flashes SOS. This eliminates false-positive confusion — a search crew seeing an SOS strobe knows it's a person in distress.
