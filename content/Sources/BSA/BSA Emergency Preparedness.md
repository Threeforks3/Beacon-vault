# BSA Emergency Preparedness

**Source:** *Emergency Preparedness*, Scouting America Merit Badge Series, 2026 printing, 96 pages. ISBN 978-0-8395-3368-9.

## Five Aspects of Emergency Preparedness

> This is what emergency preparedness is all about: preparing for, responding to, recovering from, mitigating, and preventing emergency situations. Whether an emergency involves your family or your entire community, on highways or waterways, in your home or outdoors, you can bring your Scouting skills and knowledge to the situation and help.

**Beacon relevance:** The five-aspect framework places Beacon squarely in "Respond" — the moment when an emergency is in progress and signaling matters. Beacon doesn't prevent or mitigate; it responds with light. The pamphlet's framing validates that response tools have a defined place in emergency preparation.

## Family Disaster Kit — Mandatory Signaling

The BSA-specified Family Disaster Kit includes, among its items:

> ■ Whistle to signal for help
> ■ Flashlight or lantern or chemical light sticks
> ■ Extra batteries
> ■ Battery- or crank-powered radio
> ■ Matches in waterproof container and fire starting kit

**Beacon relevance:** The whistle is explicitly designated "to signal for help" — its purpose in the kit is signaling, not recreation. Light sources are mandatory for visibility and rescue signaling. This is the same dual signaling system Beacon provides: audio (whistle integrated) and visual (strobe). The Family Disaster Kit is the civilian/home analogue to the SAR ready pack described in the Search and Rescue pamphlet — both mandate whistle and light sources for signaling.

## Personal Emergency Service Bag

> Be prepared for a mobilization call with a personal emergency service bag. You will be ready for many emergencies if you use the following checklist.

Includes: poncho, change of clothes, sleeping bag, maps, pocket knife, **flashlight**, battery-powered radio, extra batteries, personal first-aid kit, matches, emergency ration, compass, pencil/notebook, work gloves, dust masks, boots, long-sleeved shirt.

**Beacon relevance:** The personal emergency service bag is what a Scout would grab when mobilized for disaster response. The flashlight is the only active signaling tool in the bag — everything else is sustainment. Beacon adds an active visual signal (strobe) and IR detection capability to this loadout without adding weight or complexity.

## Emergency in the Outdoors — Lost or Marooned

> If you become lost or marooned with a group, be a leader. Stay calm and help others stay calm. Usually, it is best to stay put. People searching can find you easier than you can find them.

> If there is any possibility that you will have to stay out overnight, find a good campsite before dusk. Do not travel at night, except in the desert with good visibility.

**Beacon relevance:** The "stay put" doctrine is central to Beacon's use case. A lost subject who stays put is a stationary target — the ideal scenario for a strobe. Searchers move toward the light; the subject doesn't move away. The warning against traveling at night aligns with Beacon's night operation: a stationary strobe at night is the highest-contrast signal possible.

## Plane Signals — The Universal Language

> Three fires arranged in a triangle convey a universal SOS. Three of anything — visual or audible — means "distress."

> Flashing SOS using Morse code — three short flashes, three long flashes (twice as long as the short ones), three short flashes — is another way to attract help, night or day. The distress answer is two of anything.

> Signaling with a mirror can save your life, but you must know how to aim it effectively toward your rescuer.

**Beacon relevance:** This is the BSA's formal teaching on visual distress signaling — and it maps directly to Beacon's SOS strobe pattern. "Three of anything — visual or audible — means distress" is the international standard Beacon implements. The SOS Morse code flash pattern "night or day" is exactly what Beacon produces. The pamphlet validates both the pattern (SOS) and the medium (light flash) as recognized distress signals. The mirror signaling technique taught here is the same principle Beacon's overt signal mirror uses.

## Ground-to-Air Symbols

> In the daytime, a ground-level "sign language" of symbols can attract an aircraft. Because geometric figures are not found in nature, symbols such as squares and triangles will attract attention. Make the symbols big — 10 feet wide or wider — in an open area where they can be seen.

> When in doubt, use the international distress symbol, SOS.

**Beacon relevance:** Ground-to-air symbols are passive daytime signals — they require construction time and are only visible when an aircraft is overhead and looking. Beacon is the active complement: a strobe that works day or night, requires no construction, and actively catches attention rather than waiting to be noticed.

## Stalled Vehicle — Desert

> At night, attract attention any way you can: Set out an emergency light, turn on the car flashers or turn signals, leave the inside dome light on, or if possible build a fire outside.

> Use the car mirror as a signaling device as described in the "Plane Signals" section.

**Beacon relevance:** "Attract attention any way you can" — this is the emergency mindset Beacon serves. The car mirror as improvised signal mirror validates that reflective signaling is universal enough to be taught in multiple BSA pamphlets. An emergency strobe (Beacon's primary function) replaces the car flashers/dome light for a person on foot, in water, or away from a vehicle.

## Safety Rule of Three

> Do not travel alone; one buddy is good, but three or more hiking together is better. If one person gets hurt, the second can perform first aid and stay with the victim while the third can get help.
