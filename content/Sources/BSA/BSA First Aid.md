# BSA First Aid

**Source:** *First Aid*, Scouting America Merit Badge Series, 2026 printing, 108 pages. ISBN 978-0-8395-3301-8.

## Hypothermia — Cold Water Risk

> Hypothermia occurs when a person's body is losing more heat than it can generate. It is a danger for anyone who is not dressed warmly enough, although exposure to cold is seldom the only cause. Dehydration is a common contributing factor to hypothermia. Wind, rain, hunger, and exhaustion can further compound the danger.

> Temperatures do not need to be below freezing. A hiker caught out in a cool, windy rain shower without proper rain gear can be at great risk. A swimmer too far out in chilly water or a paddler who capsizes also is at risk for hypothermia.

**Beacon relevance:** BSA explicitly names "a paddler who capsizes" as a hypothermia risk — directly connecting water immersion to cold injury. This is Beacon's primary use case: a paddler in the water, unable to self-rescue, losing body heat. The faster they're found, the less hypothermia progresses. Beacon's strobe accelerates detection, which reduces immersion time, which directly reduces hypothermia severity.

## Hypothermia Progression

> A hypothermia victim may experience numbness, fatigue, irritability, slurred speech, uncontrollable shivering, poor judgment or decision making, and loss of consciousness. Shivering that stops without rewarming is a sign that the person's condition is worsening. He or she needs immediate medical care.

**Beacon relevance:** The progression from shivering → poor judgment → loss of consciousness tracks with immersion time. A victim who activates Beacon at the "uncontrollable shivering" stage gives rescuers a target before they reach "loss of consciousness" — when they can no longer assist in their own rescue.

## Hypothermia Treatment

> Treat a hypothermia victim by preventing the person from getting colder. Move the person into the shelter of a building or a tent. Remove wet clothing. Do not rewarm the person too quickly (for instance, by immersing the person in warm water); doing so can cause an irregular and dangerous heartbeat.

**Beacon relevance:** The treatment protocol emphasizes speed: prevent further cooling. Every minute a victim is in cold water deepens hypothermia. Beacon reduces search time, which reduces immersion time, which improves treatment outcomes.

## Dehydration — Cold Weather Risk

> Dehydration increases the danger of frostbite, so cold-weather travelers must be just as diligent about drinking fluids as they are when the weather is hot. Dehydration can play a significant role in a number of serious conditions, including heat exhaustion, heatstroke, hypothermia, and frostbite. Dehydration can happen in hot- and cold-weather conditions.

**Beacon relevance:** Dehydration is a compounding factor across all environmental emergencies — it accelerates hypothermia, heat exhaustion, and frostbite. A lost hiker who is dehydrated deteriorates faster. Beacon reduces time-to-find, which shortens the window during which dehydration compounds into life-threatening conditions.

## Heat Exhaustion and Heatstroke

> Heat exhaustion can be brought on by a combination of dehydration, exercise, and a warm environment. People with heat exhaustion usually complain that they feel really bad — lack of energy, generalized weakness, inattention, inability to concentrate, headache, nausea, faintness, and excessive sweating.

> Heatstroke — much more serious than heat exhaustion — can lead to death if not treated immediately. In heatstroke, the body's cooling system begins to fail and the person's core temperature rises to life-threatening levels (above 105 degrees). The first sign of true heat stroke is often collapse.

**Beacon relevance:** Heat illness impairs judgment and mobility. A subject suffering heat exhaustion can't self-rescue effectively — they become disoriented, weak, and unable to navigate. Heatstroke victims collapse. In both cases, the subject becomes stationary — exactly the condition where a strobe is most effective. The subject can't walk out, but they can be found if they're visible.

## Frostbite

> Frostbite is a condition that occurs when skin is exposed to temperatures cold enough that ice crystals begin to form in the tissues. Far from the warmth of the body's core, toes and fingers are especially vulnerable, as are the nose, ears, and cheeks.

**Beacon relevance:** Frostbite impairs fine motor control — a subject with frostbitten fingers may not be able to operate a radio, phone, or GPS. Beacon requires one button press to activate. The single-button interface is designed for this exact scenario: compromised hands in cold conditions.

## First-Aid Kit Contents

The BSA personal first-aid kit includes: gauze bandages and pads, adhesive bandages, soap, antibiotic ointment, roller bandages.

The home/patrol first-aid kit adds: adhesive tape, assorted roller bandages, triangular bandages, elastic bandages, scissors, tweezers, safety pins, non-latex disposable gloves, breathing barrier, thermometer, sterile eye dressing, instant cold pack, calamine lotion, hydrocortisone cream.

**Beacon relevance:** First-aid kits address injury — Beacon addresses the gap between injury and discovery. A victim who bandages their wound still needs someone to find them. The first-aid kit stabilizes; Beacon localizes. They're complementary tools in the same emergency timeline.
