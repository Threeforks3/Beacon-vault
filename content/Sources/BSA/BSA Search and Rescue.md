# BSA Search and Rescue Merit Badge

**Source:** *Search and Rescue*, Scouting America Merit Badge Series, 2026 printing, 100 pages. ISBN 978-0-8395-0014-8.

## SAR Doctrine for Scouts

The BSA Search and Rescue pamphlet is a formal training document that teaches Scouts the structure, tactics, and personal preparedness required for SAR operations. While written for youth, it reflects real SAR doctrine — the Incident Command System, search tactics, gear requirements, and safety protocols that professional SAR teams use.

## Incident Command System

> The Incident Command System (ICS) is a systematic approach to the management of emergency incidents. Used by fire departments, emergency medical services, law enforcement agencies, and search and rescue teams to manage all types of emergencies, this system is flexible and scalable to all types and sizes of incidents and events.

> In 2003, Homeland Security Presidential Directive 5 (HSPD-5) mandated that all federal agencies use ICS to manage all incidents.

**Beacon relevance:** ICS is the operational backbone of every SAR mission. Beacon operates at the tactical edge — the searcher on the ground, the subject in the water — but the framework that deploys that searcher is ICS. Understanding ICS means understanding the chain that Beacon plugs into.

## SAR Organizations Named

> Members of search and rescue teams are nearly all volunteers. The Air Force Rescue Coordination Center serves as the single agency responsible for coordinating land-based federal SAR activities in the 48 contiguous states. Search and rescue is one of the United States Coast Guard's oldest missions. The National Association for Search and Rescue (NASAR) is a nonprofit organization that promotes development and improved coordination among all SAR resources. The Mountain Rescue Association (MRA), National Ski Patrol, dive teams, cave rescue groups, and four-wheel drive clubs all stand ready to assist with SAR.

**Beacon relevance:** The SAR ecosystem is a coalition of volunteer organizations coordinated by federal agencies. BSA explicitly names USCG, NASAR, MRA, and CAP — the same organizations the Beacon coalition targets.

## Search vs. Rescue

> A search is an emergency situation requiring a team of trained searchers to locate a missing person. A rescue is an emergency situation where a person's location is known — perhaps having just been found by searchers — and he or she must be removed from danger and returned to safety.

**Beacon relevance:** Beacon addresses the *search* phase — making the subject findable. It does not perform rescue. This matches the SAR community's understanding that finding and extracting are separate operations with separate tools.

## SAR Team Ready Pack — Gear Requirements

> Search team members are called out on very short notice, so having a pack ready to go, called a ready pack, is important. Most search teams require their members to have sufficient clothing and gear to enable them to stay in the field for at least 24 hours.

The BSA-specified SAR team gear list includes:

**Clothing:**
- Sturdy hiking boots, work gloves, head cover, waterproof/windproof outer layer
- "Cotton kills" — wool or synthetic only

**Mandatory gear:**
- Food for 24 hours (high caloric)
- Water (2 quarts minimum)
- Swiss Army knife or multitool
- Fire starter
- Compass with 5° accuracy
- Map of search area
- **Whistle**
- **Signal mirror**
- **Two light sources** (flashlight and/or headlamp, plus extra batteries and replacement bulbs)
- Personal first-aid kit
- Space blanket
- Pencil/pen and waterproof paper
- 20 feet of 1-inch tubular nylon webbing

**Optional gear:**
- Safety equipment, generally including an ANSI Class 2 vest

**Beacon relevance:** The BSA's SAR gear list is a direct source of truth for what professional searchers carry. Both a whistle and a signal mirror are mandatory — not optional, not recommended, mandatory. Two light sources are mandatory. This is the SAR community's own doctrine saying: when you go to find someone, you bring signaling tools. Beacon combines the mirror, the strobe (one of the two light sources), and adds IR detection that no ready pack includes. A SAR team member equipped per BSA standards would immediately recognize Beacon as filling their signaling category — and adding a capability (IR strobe) they don't carry.

## Passive Search Tactics — Attracting Lookouts

> A passive tactic that is often effective is an attracting lookout. A two-person team is sent to a high location to try to spot the subject's campfire or tent, or another sign that the subject may be in the area. The team may build a campfire or use strobe lights, whistles, air horns, sirens, or other signals in the hope that the subject will respond.

**Beacon relevance:** This is a critical passage. BSA SAR doctrine explicitly names **strobe lights** as a passive search tactic — used by searchers to attract a subject's attention, and by extension, employed by subjects to attract searchers. Beacon is exactly this: a strobe light that the subject carries, which turns passive (waiting to be found) into active (signaling to be found). The pamphlet validates the tactic from the searcher side; Beacon performs it from the subject side.

## Sound Sweeps

> To conduct a sound sweep, each searcher must have a radio and a whistle. They space themselves along the search area boundary at a distance of 500 to 600 feet. The team leader may transmit "whistle blast in five seconds" and then count down "5-4-3-2-1." All searchers blow their whistles simultaneously and then listen for a response from the subject.

**Beacon relevance:** Sound sweeps are the audio equivalent of what Beacon does visually. Multiple searchers coordinate a simultaneous signal (whistle blast) and listen for response. Beacon does the same in the visual domain — a coordinated strobe pattern that searchers look for. The 500-600 foot spacing translates to visual search spacing: searchers spread at that distance would need to see a light source at 250-300 feet minimum — well within Beacon's visual range.

## Night Search Doctrine

> Night searches are very difficult and present added hazards. Advantages: dogs work better at night, tracks show up better when illuminated by flashlights, footprints do not dry up as quickly, human voices carry farther, and subjects usually hole up rather than travel at night.

> Disadvantages: searchers have an increased risk of accidents, it is more difficult to transport a subject if found, clues may be destroyed or missing, lights hamper searchers' night vision, and it is difficult for the team leader to see and guide team members.

**Beacon relevance:** The night search advantages cascade directly to Beacon: subjects hole up (stationary = predictable position), voices carry farther (a subject who hears searchers can activate Beacon as response), and the strobe is visible at ranges far exceeding voice. The disadvantages — lights hampering night vision — actually favor Beacon: searchers *want* to see a light at night; it's their own flashlights that degrade natural night vision. A subject's strobe is a signal, not an impediment.

## Probability of Detection (POD)

> POD is usually expressed as a percentage value, i.e., "we searched our assigned area with a 50 percent POD." In a wilderness setting, achieving a 100 percent POD is next to impossible, but the goal is to always search — and sometimes re-search — all areas of high probability until a POD of at least 85 percent to 95 percent is reached.

**Beacon relevance:** POD quantifies what Beacon improves. A subject without signaling capability contributes nothing to POD — searchers must visually detect a human form. A subject with a strobe increases POD because the detection cue changes from "find a person" to "find a flashing light." The strobe is a high-contrast, active signal that pushes POD toward the 85-95% target faster than unaided visual search.

## Clue Categories

> Clues can be categorized into six broad areas: Physical (footprints, scent), People (witnesses), Recorded (trail register, trip plan), Event (a light, campfire, signal, human voice), Investigative, Analytical.

**Beacon relevance:** Under BSA SAR doctrine, a strobe flash is an **Event clue** — "a light, signal." When a searcher sees a flashing light, they have an Event clue directing them to the subject. This is the doctrinal category Beacon operates in: it creates Event clues on demand.

## Critical Separation

> How far team members are spaced apart and still able to search effectively is called critical separation. Place an object on the ground that bears some relation to the size of the subject being sought. The team walks around the object so that it is kept in view. The distance between two opposite searchers is the critical separation.

**Beacon relevance:** Critical separation is determined by visual detection of a human-scale object. A strobe light effectively multiplies critical separation — searchers can detect a flashing light at far greater distance than a stationary human form. This means fewer searchers can cover the same area, or the same searchers achieve higher POD.

## Becoming the Subject

> Here are some pointers to keep from becoming the subject of a search and rescue: Always have a trip plan and share it with your parent or guardian. Stick to your trip plan. Know what the weather is like. Never hike or camp alone. Have proper gear and clothing.

**Beacon relevance:** BSA's prevention doctrine centers on the trip plan — the same concept the SAR vault uses as the first dimension on every C Outdoor Enthusiasts page. The pamphlet reinforces that the most effective SAR tool is preparation. Beacon fits in the "proper gear" category — a signaling device that turns a prepared subject into a findable subject.
