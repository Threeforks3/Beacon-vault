# MRA Avalanche Facts

**Source:** Alpine Rescue Team — Avalanche Awareness factsheet. Fully accredited MRA member since 1962.

## Survival Statistics

> Someone completely buried in an avalanche has a 90% chance of survival if they're uncovered within the first 15 minutes (absent any life threatening trauma-related injuries); someone buried 30 minutes has approximately a 50% chance of survival. The chances of survival drop dramatically after 30 minutes.

**Beacon relevance:** The 15-minute survival window is the companion rescue window — the victim's party, not organized SAR, performs the recovery. An avalanche transceiver gets within 2-3 meters. A strobe confirms the exact burial position when rescuers break through the snow surface. Beacon is the final visual confirmation layer in the transceiver→probe→shovel→strobe chain.

## Companion Rescue Priority

> Very few people, caught in an avalanche, have been able to dig themselves out. For someone completely buried, their survival depends solely on the quick actions of the other members in their party.

> "If my partner's caught in a slide, should I immediately go for help?" NO! You are that person's BEST chance for survival. Do everything possible to locate the subject, and then go for help.

**Beacon relevance:** The MRA doctrine is explicit — companion rescue first, organized rescue second. This means the signaling equipment must be usable by untrained companions, not just SAR professionals. Beacon's single-button activation and SOS pattern are designed for this: the companion activates it, places it at the dig site, and the strobe marks the position for helicopter NVG if organized rescue arrives during the dig.

## Required Equipment

> Avalanche beacons, shovels and probe poles and the knowledge of how to use them are essential pieces of equipment for anyone traveling in avalanche terrain.

**Beacon relevance:** The standard kit is transceiver + probe + shovel. No strobe. Beacon is a fourth layer — not replacing any of the three, but adding active visual/IR signaling for the moment rescuers break through the snow surface and for helicopter detection through suspended snow dust.

## Key Statistics

- Most avalanches occur on slopes of 30-to-45 degrees, during or following a major snow event
- Most victims have some avalanche training and consider themselves skilled
- Colorado: ~6 avalanche fatalities/year, approximately 1/3 of all US avalanche deaths
- Major snow event: 1"/hour for several hours, or 10" in 24 hours
- 15-50 mph winds can elevate danger dramatically after as little as an hour — even without new snow

## Jurisdiction

> The local county sheriff is responsible for and coordinates ALL backcountry emergency responses including (non ski-area) avalanches.
