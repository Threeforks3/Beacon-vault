# MRA Search and Rescue for Outdoor Leaders

**Source:** "Search and Rescue for Outdoor Leaders" by Charley Shimanski, Mountain Rescue Association, 2008. Developed for experiential education programs that may be required to respond to SAR emergencies.

## Lost Person Behavior

> A summary of studies on the behavior of lost persons includes:
> - If a lost person finds a trail, they might get on it and run like hell — convinced they're on the way back to their last point, when in fact they might be running FROM it
> - Rarely will a lost person reverse their direction on a trail
> - Many people ignore trails and follow their own logic — traveling in a straight line. They figure they'll come to a road or highway — not expecting the cliff or impassable river that ultimately confronts them
> - Some lost people will climb/hike to the top of the closest hill to get a better view, only to find that the trees atop that hill obstruct any view
> - The majority of lost people will travel downhill and/or downstream
> - Many lost people will travel at night — even without a flashlight

**Beacon relevance:** Lost person behavior directly contradicts the "stay put" doctrine that SAR depends on. Lost people move — at night, without a flashlight. The search area expands with every hour. A strobe on the lost person's pack or hung from a branch provides a fixed signal even if they continue to move. It also addresses the night travel finding: a person who will travel without a flashlight is invisible to ground searchers but a strobe on their person makes them actively visible.

## The Stay Put Doctrine

> "Stay put" is the most important lesson you need to give your program participants. Unless there is imminent threat of life or limb, any lost person is better off if they do not move. This will keep the search area small.

> "A number of lost student incidents have been unnecessarily aggravated because students did not know what to do when separated from the patrol or instructor." — Colorado Outward Bound School's Course Director Manual

**Beacon relevance:** The standard wilderness lost hiker advice — "hug a tree" — becomes advice backed by a 2+ mile strobe. If the lost person stays put AND activates a strobe, the search transitions from grid search to "fly toward the flashing light."

## Clue-Based Search

> The truth is that professional searchers are looking more for clues than for victims. There are always more clues than victims, and once clues are found, the size of the search area is significantly reduced.

> To make the job of searchers easier, lost parties can leave clues such as clear footprints, arrows drawn with sticks, notes attached to trees or anything they can think of to signal rescuers.

**Beacon relevance:** An active strobe is the ultimate clue — it doesn't need to be found, it announces itself. Compared to footprints (degrade in rain), arrows (require the searcher to look down), and notes (require the searcher to stop and read), a strobe visible from 2+ nautical miles through canopy gaps is a clue that finds the searcher.

## Search Area Expansion

> Little is lost when calling for help. A search area expands exponentially with each hour a person is lost. Call for help — even if you end up not needing it.

> "Urgent response to a lost person incident reduces the size of the search area" — Managing the Lost Person Incident, NASAR

**Beacon relevance:** The exponential search area expansion is the core argument for immediate signaling. A 26-hour strobe activated at the moment of distress keeps the effective search area at zero — the search aircraft flies toward the signal rather than expanding a grid.

## Attraction Techniques

> The base camp might be a good location to practice attraction techniques — build a smoky fire, make noise, honk car horns, etc.

**Beacon relevance:** Every attraction technique listed is either time-limited (fire burns out), range-limited (noise carries ~0.1 mile in forest), or infrastructure-dependent (car horn requires a car). A strobe at 19,000+ mcd with 360° coverage and 26-hour runtime is a continuous attraction technique that requires no ongoing effort from the victim.

## Search Methodology

> - All search teams must be at least 2 people
> - Start documenting early. The person in charge of documentation should clearly mark search assignments and departure times on a map
> - Designate the search areas with the highest probability and send searchers there immediately
> - Start a "hasty search" early on — have people run the trails and streams, etc., carrying a whistle and periodically stopping to blow it 3 times
> - As soon as possible, a "scent article" should be collected for search dogs
> - Leave notes at trailheads or other exit points telling the lost party what to do if they get there

## MRA Jurisdiction and Cost

> The Mountain Rescue Association, representing thousands of rescue mountaineers nationwide, does not charge for its services.

> In the United States, search and rescue generally falls under the jurisdiction of the county sheriff or state police.
